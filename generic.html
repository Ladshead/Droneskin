For this project we set out to create a miniature for the Table Top Role Playing Game (TTRPG) Dungeons & Dragons (D&D). The goal of the project was to have a miniature that could be used to represent an Ancient Red Dragon at a game table. We based our design off of the Ancient Red Dragon illustration from the D&D Monster Manual.

When designing this piece we had to consider the environment in which it would be used. TTRPG's, as the name suggests, are played at a table. This means that games usually take place internally, under artificial lights, and within low impact environments. These conditions influenced the choices we made regarding materials; Since the product wasn't going to be exposed to sunlight, we weren't limited to UV resistant materials, such as acrylic or polyetherimide. We also didn't need an impact resistant resistant material, such as nylon, as in its intended setting it would not be used roughly.

3D scanning hardware, software, and workshop facilities

For 3D scanning we used a HandyScan 3D with VX elements, this allowed us to get an accurate and detailed digital representation by circling the model while scanning. One issue that arose in this stage was that the position of the model had it leaning on one wing, this obscured a large section of its body and meant that the wing deformed slightly while the weight of the dragon was resting on it. Due to this, and the detail of the model, it took forty minutes and multiple scans, which were then edited together, to get a full 3D scan. The deformation of the wing also caused issues with the combining of the scans, as the wing returned to its usual place when the dragon was placed in a different position. This issue meant that combining the model took more time and care.

3D modelling software

Left & Right

The dragon was shaped using Blender, a tool known for its sculpting features. Rough edges were smoothed, and imperfections were fixed to create a clean look. Then, we adjusted the dragon to match our idea of an ancient red dragon. Once we were happy with the design in Blender, we moved it to Fusion 360 to ensure it was the right size. We tweaked its dimensions to fit our planned scale and checked that everything looked good before moving forward.

3D printing hardware and workshop facilities

After finalising the design, the model was prepared for printing by exporting it as an STL file, ensuring compatibility with 3D printing software. The file was then transmitted to the 3D printing facility where PLA filament was selected to bring the envisioned design to life.

Due to the scale of the model it had to be printed in sections. The first section to be printed was the head, this was made up of a top and a bottom jaw that were printed separately.

After this, the body was printed. The body was the largest section of the body, as it contained the torso, legs, and wings. When printing this section, there were some misprints on the wings. These misprints took the form of wire-like bundles of PLA, where some of the wing spikes should have been. These errors were not overly concerning for this model, as the bundles could be removed and it could be painted to look like battle damage, but in future printings, care would be taken to ensure these spikes are properly rendered.

Finally, the tail was printed in two parts; A short section that connected to the body, and a longer segment that would be attached with a mechanism in order to swivel left to right. This section was printed without error.

One issue that was present once each section had been printed was that the body was scaled differently from the head and tail. This meant that there was a lip where the head and tail connected to the body. When printing this piece again, care will have to be taken to ensure the scale matches.
